A little bit about me...



I work in the Michigan State University Department of Physics
and Astronomy, where I've been on the faculty since 2009.

My research is in the field of high energy physics, which is the study
of the most basic building blocks of the universe and the fundamental
forces through which they interact. This field is also commonly known
as elementary particle physics. Scientists working in this field explore
the most fundamental questions about the nature of our universe.

I perform my research at the highest energy particle accelerator in
the world along with colleagues from both MSU and around the world.
In particular, my work focuses on understanding the physical
mechanisms that explain why matter has mass. While mass and its
interaction with gravity is the most clear indication that forces exist,
the mystery of why particles have mass at all and why observed particle
masses span such a great range remains a major focus in high energy physics.

I also have interests in extentions to our current theoretical models that
predict new, heavy gauge bosons (eg, W-/Z-prime models). These models can provide
an explanation for why we observe puzzling results in our relatively low-energy
universe, by providing a very high-energy new physics mechanism.

The ATLAS Experiment

The ATLAS experiment collects data from proton collisions inside the Large Hadron Collider, which is located at the CERN research facility just outside Geneva, Switzerland. This is where my research team and MSU colleagues work. We collaborate with physicists from universities and laboratories around the world.

Big Data for Big Questions

The data we collect allow us to test whether our predictions for how particles interact are accurate, or if there is something else happening when we collide protons together. The collisions produce a dense spray of particles, which we detect with the ATLAS experimental apparatus (this is all illustrated on the right). The proton collisions occur every 25 nanoseconds, so our data sample is truly BIG and grows every day.

About my research

My professional interests cover a wide range of activities. Here are a few highlights.

  • Searches for Exotic Matter

    My research group has been focusing lately on searches for exotic heavy resonances, which have never been seen before. You can find links to our research here.

  • Teaching

    I am priveledged to be able to teach physics to both undergraduate and graduate students at MSU. You can find links to the courses that I teach here.

  • Statistics in HEP

    Most HEP results culminate in a statistical analysis of the data. Improving our methods and solving common problems is a fundamental need in our field.

  • LHC Upgrades

    We periodically upgrade the ATLAS detector to adapt to increased energy and/or collision intensity regimes. You can find links to the MSU contributions to ATLAS upgrades here.

  • Machine Learning

    ATLAS data is both BIG (lots of it!) and feature rich. This is a magnet for machine learning enthusiasts. Have a look here at what's happening at ATLAS in ML.

  • Physics Outreach

    Communication to non-scientists about particle physics is an important part in the future success of our field. You can find links to HEP outreach here.
